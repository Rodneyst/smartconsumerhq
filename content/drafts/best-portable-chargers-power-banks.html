Power banks have become essential travel and daily carry items — and the gap between a good one and a mediocre one is now significant. A poorly engineered power bank might advertise 20,000mAh but deliver 12,000mAh in usable capacity due to cheap cells and inefficient conversion circuits. It might claim 65W output but throttle to 18W under sustained load. It might charge your phone in 45 minutes on the spec sheet but take 90 minutes in practice because the fast charging protocol handshake doesn't work correctly with your specific phone. The marketing in this category is particularly misleading because most buyers have no way to verify the specs without testing equipment.

How to Choose a Portable Charger or Power Bank

Two questions frame the decision: What are you charging? And how long do you need to power it between wall outlet access?

1. Capacity: How Much Do You Actually Need?

More mAh is not always better — it means more weight. A daily commuter who might be caught at 20% battery needs 5,000–10,000mAh. A weekend traveler who wants insurance against one dead-battery day needs 10,000–20,000mAh. A business traveler spending multiple days without reliable outlet access needs 20,000–25,000mAh+. Carrying a 25,600mAh power bank daily because "more is better" means carrying 1.2 extra pounds every day for capacity you'll rarely use. Match capacity to your real use case rather than buying maximum capacity as default.

2. Output Wattage: Phone vs. Tablet vs. Laptop Requirements

Phone fast charging requires 18W–45W depending on the fast charging standard. Tablets charge at 18W–30W. Thin-and-light laptops charge at 45W–65W. MacBook Pro and high-performance laptops charge at 67W–140W. A power bank's maximum output wattage determines what it can charge effectively, not just whether it can charge it. A 20W power bank will trickle-charge a laptop over 6+ hours where a 65W power bank charges the same laptop in 1.5 hours. Verify the output wattage matches your primary charging device before buying.

3. Charging Protocols: USB-PD vs. Qualcomm Quick Charge

USB Power Delivery (USB-PD) is the universal fast charging standard used by iPhones, MacBooks, and most modern devices. Qualcomm Quick Charge (QC) is used by many Android phones. The best power banks support both. The Anker 737, INIU, and Baseus all support both USB-PD and QC. Check which protocol your phone uses — a power bank that only supports QC won't fast charge an iPhone; one that only supports USB-PD won't fast charge a Qualcomm-chipset Android at full speed. Most quality power banks over $35 support both.

4. Portability: Size and Weight vs. Capacity Trade-offs

Power bank weight roughly scales with capacity. The Anker 622 MagSafe is 4.3 oz and pocket-sized. The Mophie 10,000mAh is 7 oz — slim enough for a jacket pocket. The INIU 25,600mAh and Baseus 20,000mAh are around 1.1–1.2 lbs — they need a bag. The Anker 737 at 1.5 lbs belongs in a larger travel bag. Daily carry users should prioritize under 0.5 lbs (roughly 10,000mAh maximum at current cell density). Travel users with bag space can go higher. Never compromise on which charger you'll actually bring with you — a 25,600mAh charger you leave at home because it's too heavy helps nobody.

5. Simultaneous Charging and Power Distribution Intelligence

Most multi-port power banks split maximum output between active ports — when two devices are charging simultaneously, each gets a fraction of the total rated output. The Anker 737 and Baseus are specifically engineered to prioritize the highest-wattage-requiring device (typically a laptop) and route remaining power to secondary ports. If you regularly charge a laptop and phone simultaneously from one power bank, power distribution intelligence is worth prioritizing. If you primarily charge one device at a time, it's irrelevant.

5 Common Power Bank Mistakes Buyers Make

1. Buying a cheap power bank that delivers 60% of its advertised capacity

The most widespread deception in the power bank market. A "20,000mAh" power bank from an unknown brand frequently delivers 12,000–14,000mAh in real-world use because the cells are lower quality, the conversion circuit is inefficient, and the mAh rating reflects cell capacity at the raw voltage rather than usable output voltage. The 15,000+ verified reviews on the INIU and 8,000+ on the Anker 737 provide real-world capacity validation that no-name brands don't have. Stick to brands with large verified review pools or independent testing data.

2. Buying based on mAh alone without checking output wattage

A 20,000mAh power bank with 18W maximum output will slowly charge your laptop at the same speed a standard phone charger would. Check the output wattage first for your primary charging device, then choose the capacity you need within models that meet the wattage requirement. The INIU at 65W and 25,600mAh costs the same as some 15W options at 20,000mAh — always verify wattage alongside capacity.

3. Not checking airline compatibility before travel

Power banks in checked luggage are prohibited by TSA and IATA worldwide — always carry-on only. Power banks over 100Wh (approximately 27,000mAh) require airline approval in advance; over 160Wh are prohibited on passenger flights entirely. Check your power bank's Wh rating (printed on the label or in the specs) before any air travel. All five models in this guide are within the unrestricted carry-on limit of 100Wh.

4. Using the wrong cable for fast charging

A USB-C cable is not a USB-C cable in terms of charging capability. A charging cable that doesn't support the fast charging protocol (USB-PD or QC) will charge at the default 5V/2A rate regardless of how capable the power bank and device are. Always use a cable rated for the wattage you need: USB-PD 65W requires a cable rated for 5A (usually labeled on the packaging). Cheap bundled cables frequently limit charging to 15W–25W even when both the power bank and device support faster rates. Invest in a quality cable rated for your maximum charging wattage.

5. Storing a power bank at 100% or 0% for extended periods

Leaving a lithium power bank fully charged or fully depleted for weeks damages the cells and reduces long-term capacity. The optimal storage charge is 40–60%. If you're packing a power bank for a trip, charge it the day before rather than weeks in advance. If you won't use a power bank for more than a month, discharge it to approximately 50% before storage. This single habit can double the battery's effective lifespan in terms of retained capacity over time.
