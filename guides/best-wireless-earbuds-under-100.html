The under-$100 wireless earbud category has become genuinely good. Three years ago, the trade-offs at this price point were severe — poor ANC that barely reduced wind noise, call quality that made you sound like you were calling from a fish tank, battery life measured in hours rather than a full day's use, and fit that was reliable only while sitting still. Today, Sony, Jabra, Anker, Samsung, and JLab have all pushed real engineering into the sub-$100 tier. The result is a category where the best options deliver sound quality, ANC performance, and call clarity that were $200–$250 features two years ago.

That said, the gap between the best and the average in this category is still large. For every Sony WF-C710N or Jabra Elite 4 that punches above its price, there are a dozen models with 4-star Amazon ratings that deliver mediocre audio, inconsistent connectivity, and ANC that does little more than dampen the highest-pitched frequencies. Distinguishing between them requires looking past the spec sheet — codec support, microphone configuration, driver size, and ear tip quality all affect real-world performance in ways that aren't obvious from product listings.

We compared over 20 wireless earbuds under $100, examining audio codec support, measured ANC effectiveness, call quality based on verified reviewer data, battery life in continuous use vs. advertised specs, Bluetooth connectivity stability, and IP water resistance ratings. The result is five picks that cover every primary use case at this budget: the best all-around performer, the clear leader for calls, the standout for battery life, the best choice for Samsung users, and the strongest budget ANC option for those spending under $45.

How to Choose Wireless Earbuds Under $100

Two questions determine which earbuds are right for you: What do you primarily use them for (music, calls, exercise, commuting)? And what device are you pairing them with? The answers filter out most of the comparison immediately.

1. ANC: When It Matters and When to Skip It

If you commute by public transit, work in a noisy open office, or fly regularly, ANC is worth paying for. The consistent low-frequency noise of trains, planes, and HVAC responds well to ANC at this price tier. If you primarily use earbuds at home in a quiet environment, skip ANC and put the budget toward better drivers and audio quality instead. The Sony WF-C710N and Anker Liberty 4 NC offer the best ANC under $100; the JLab GO Air ANC offers functional ANC for users who need isolation but can't reach $60.

2. Codec Support: SBC, AAC, aptX, and LDAC Explained

Bluetooth audio codec determines the maximum audio quality transmitted from your device. SBC is the universal baseline — supported on all devices, acceptable quality. AAC is what Apple devices use — noticeably better on iPhone and iPad. aptX is Qualcomm's higher-quality codec for Android. LDAC is Sony's highest-quality codec, transmitting at 990kbps (versus 345kbps for aptX) — only meaningful on Android with high-quality audio sources. If you use an iPhone, prioritize AAC support; if you use Android with a streaming service in high quality mode, LDAC (Anker Liberty 4 NC) makes a perceptible difference.

3. Fit Security: In-Canal vs. Wing-Tip for Exercise Use

Standard in-canal earbuds (Sony, Jabra, Anker) rely entirely on ear tip seal for fit. This works well for stationary use and moderate activity but can fail during running, HIIT, or heavy head movement. Wing-tip designs (Samsung Galaxy Buds FE, and JLab options) add a silicone hook that anchors in the ear's antihelix, providing a secondary fit point. If you exercise with earbuds more than 3 times per week, wing-tip stability is worth prioritizing. If you primarily use them for work and commuting, standard in-canal fit is fine and typically more comfortable for extended sitting.

4. Call Quality: What to Prioritize for WFH and Commutes

For remote workers, call quality is the most important spec after sound. The key factors are microphone count and noise filtering algorithm quality — not just microphone count. Three microphones with poor filtering algorithms produce worse calls than two microphones with professional-grade filtering. Jabra's background noise filtering is the most sophisticated at this price point because it's derived from professional headset technology. If you're on calls for more than 2 hours per day, the Jabra Elite 4 is the clear recommendation regardless of how you rank its other features.

5. Multipoint Connection: Switching Between Devices

Multipoint connection allows earbuds to be simultaneously connected to two or three devices, automatically switching audio to whichever device is playing. This is increasingly important for users with a phone and work laptop who don't want to manually reconnect earbuds when switching between devices. The Jabra Elite 4 supports 3-device multipoint — the most in this comparison. Sony WF-C710N supports 2-device. The JLab and Samsung Buds FE support standard single-device connection with manual switching. If seamless device switching is a daily use case, weight this feature accordingly in your decision.

5 Common Wireless Earbud Mistakes Buyers Make

1. Buying based on rated battery hours without checking ANC-on specs

Battery life specs are always listed at maximum (ANC off, low volume). The Sony WF-C710N is listed as "10 hours" — but that's without ANC. With ANC enabled at normal volume, it delivers 7.5 hours. This distinction matters because most ANC users have ANC on most of the time. Always find the ANC-on battery rating specifically when comparing earbuds you intend to use with ANC regularly.

2. Not testing the included tip sizes

The most common reason for poor sound quality and weak ANC performance is a bad ear tip seal. If the ear tips don't form a proper in-ear seal, bass frequencies escape and the ANC microphone calibration reads ambient noise incorrectly. Spend 5 minutes testing each included tip size in each ear independently — they're often different sizes for different ears — before concluding that the sound is disappointing. Foam tips (available as aftermarket replacements for $8–$12) solve persistent fit issues for most users.

3. Over-prioritizing brand name over current generation specs

Brand name earbuds from 2022 at $80 now compete with no-name earbuds from 2026 at $40 that have better specs on paper. Conversely, a well-known brand's budget tier product often uses older drivers and processors than their previous generation mid-tier. Check the release date and compare the specs of what's actually in the box, not just the brand association. The Anker Soundcore Liberty 4 NC outperforms many $120+ earbuds from 2023 on battery and LDAC codec support.

4. Using earbuds for phone calls in noisy environments without testing microphone first

Most earbuds sound adequate for microphone quality during quiet in-home testing. The real test is a phone call on a busy street or in a café. Before relying on any earbuds for important calls, test the call quality in your actual working environment — not just at your desk at home. Budget earbuds that seem fine in silence often fail visibly when wind, street noise, or HVAC are present.

5. Ignoring software support and app quality

A companion app transforms how useful an earbud is over its lifetime — EQ customization, ANC level control, custom touch controls, and firmware updates that fix bugs and add features. The Soundcore app (Anker), Sound+ app (Jabra), and Headphones app (Sony) are all well-designed and actively maintained. An earbud without a companion app locks you into the factory-tuned sound profile and default controls for the entire product lifespan. This matters most for users with specific sound preferences or hearing profiles.
